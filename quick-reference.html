ACLS Core Algorithms

Step-by-step decision pathways from AHA 2020 Guidelines (2023 focused update).

🫀 Adult Cardiac Arrest — Universal Algorithm

1

Recognize cardiac arrest

Check responsiveness → Activate emergency response → Get AED/defibrillator → Check pulse ≤10 s

2

Start CPR (30:2)

Compressions: depth ≥2 inches, rate 100–120/min. Minimize interruptions. Place defibrillator pads.

3

Check rhythm → VF/pVT vs PEA/asystole?

VF / Pulseless VT (Shockable)
  1. Shock then immediately resume CPR
  2. CPR × 2 min → establish IV/IO
  3. CPR × 2 min → epinephrine 1 mg q3–5 min
  4. CPR × 2 min → shock
  5. CPR × 2 min → amiodarone 300 mg
  6. CPR × 2 min → shock
  7. CPR × 2 min → amiodarone 150 mg
  8. Repeat shock → epi cycle
PEA / Asystole (Non-Shockable)
  1. Do NOT shock
  2. CPR × 2 min → establish IV/IO
  3. CPR × 2 min → epinephrine 1 mg q3–5 min
  4. CPR × 2 min → re-evaluate
  5. Consider Hs & Ts (below)
4

Search for Reversible Causes (H's & T's)

Hypovolemia → fluids, blood

Hypoxia → secure airway, ventilate

Hydrogen ion (acidosis) → ventilation, NaHCO₃ if severe

Hypo/Hyperkalemia → K⁺ replacement or Ca²⁺/insulin/glucose

Hypothermia → rewarm

Tension pneumothorax → needle decompression

Tamponade, cardiac → pericardiocentesis

Toxins → specific antidotes

Thrombosis, pulmonary → consider tPA

Thrombosis, coronary → emergent cath

🏥 Post-Cardiac Arrest Care

Ventilatory Targets

  • • SpO₂ 92–98%
  • • PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg
  • • Avoid hyperventilation
  • • Consider advanced airway early

Hemodynamic Targets

  • • MAP ≥ 65 mmHg
  • • Consider vasopressors if hypotensive
  • • 12-lead ECG for STEMI → cath lab
  • • Obtain point-of-care ultrasound

Neurologic Care

  • • Targeted temperature management 32–36°C × 24 h
  • • Continuous EEG monitoring
  • • Treat seizures aggressively
  • • No prophylactic anticonvulsants

Coronary Reperfusion

  • • STEMI on ECG → emergent PCI
  • • No STEMI + hemodynamic/electrical instability → consider PCI
  • • No STEMI + stable → early coronary angiography (within 2–6 h)

⚗️ Warfarin

Mechanism: Vitamin K antagonist (inhibits synthesis of II, VII, IX, X, proteins C & S)

Starting dose: 5 mg daily (2.5 mg if elderly/frail)

Target INR: 2–3 most indications; 2.5–3.5 mechanical mitral valve

Monitoring: INR weekly initially → monthly when stable

Interactions: Major — amiodarone (↑INR), antibiotics (↑INR), NSAIDs, CYP450 inducers/inhibitors

Reversal: Vitamin K (oral or IV 5–10 mg) + 4-factor PCC (25–50 U/kg)

💉 Heparins

UFH bolus: 80 U/kg IV → 18 U/kg/hr infusion (aPTT goal 1.5–2.5×

Enoxaparin (LMWH): 1 mg/kg SC q12h (therapeutic) — renally adjust if CrCl <30

Fondaparinux: 5–10 mg SC daily (based on weight)

UFH reversal: Protamine sulfate 1 mg per 100 U UFH (max 50 mg)

Enoxaparin reversal: Protamine 1 mg per 1 mg enoxaparin (partial reversal only)

Rapid Assessment Checklists

One-page checklists for common cardiovascular scenarios.

🚑 STEMI — ED Management Checklist

🔴 IMMEDIATE (<10 min)

  • ☐ 12-lead ECG — diagnose STEMI criteria met?
  • ☐ Activates code STEMI / cath lab
  • ☐ Aspirin 325 mg chewed
  • ☐ O2 only if SpO2 <90%
  • ☐ IV access × 2, cardiac monitor
  • ☐ Troponin, CBC, BMP, coags, lipid panel
  • ☐ NTG 0.4 mg SL q5 min × 3 (if BP allows)
  • ☐ Morphine (for refractory pain)

🟢 EARLY (Cath lab <90 min)

  • ☐ P2Y12 inhibitor: ticagrelor 180 mg or clopidogrel 600 mg load
  • ☐ Anticoagulation: UFH bolus or enoxaparin
  • ☐ High-intensity statin: atorvastatin 80 mg
  • ☐ Beta-blocker if no contraindication (metoprolol 5 mg IV)
  • ☐ Door-to-balloon time <90 min (PCI)
  • ☐ If fibrinolytics: start within 30 min of arrival
  • ☐ ECHO when stable

💓 New-Onset Atrial Fibrillation

Step 1: Assess Stability

Unstable (shock, ischemic chest pain, acute HF, altered mental status)?

→ YES: Immediate synchronized cardioversion

→ NO: Proceed to rate vs rhythm control assessment

Step 2: Rate Control (if stable)

  • ☐ Beta-blocker: metoprolol 5 mg IV or esmolol infusion
  • ☐ Diltiazem: 0.25 mg/kg IV → 5–15 mg/hr drip
  • ☐ Avoid non-DHP CCB in HFrEF
  • ☐ Target HR <110 bpm (RACE II lenient control)

Step 3: Anticoagulation

  • ☐ Calculate CHADS₂-VASc
  • ☐ Score ≥2 M / ≥3 F → DOAC
  • ☐ Score 1 → shared decision
  • ☐ >48 h onset or unknown → TEE first or anticoagulate × 3 wk

Step 4: Cardioversion (if rhythm control chosen)

☐ Duration <48 h: cardiovert directly (still consider TEE if high stroke risk)

☐ Duration >48 h or unknown: TEE-guided or anticoagulate 3 wk before cardioversion

☐ Post-cardioversion anticoagulation: minimum 4 weeks regardless of CHADS₂-VASc

🔍 Aortic Dissection — Rapid Workup

Red Flag Symptoms

  • ☐ "Tearing" chest/back pain (sudden onset)
  • ☐ Pulse deficit between arms
  • ☐ Blood pressure differential >20 mmHg between arms
  • ☐ New diastolic murmur (AR) — aortic regurgitation
  • ☐ Neurologic deficit (stroke-like)
  • ☐ Widened mediastinum on CXR

Diagnostic Steps

  • ☐ CT angiography (gold std if stable)
  • ☐ TEE (if unstable or at bedside)
  • ☐ CXR (widened mediastinum, displaced calcification)
  • ☐ Stanford A → emergent surgery
  • ☐ Stanford B → medical management (BP control)

Medical Management (Both Types)

  • ☐ Beta-blocker first (esmolol drip): target HR <60 bpm
  • ☐ Add vasodilator (nitroprusside) after beta-blockade: target SBP 100–120 mmHg
  • ☐ NEVER give vasodilator alone (reflex tachycardia ↑dP/dt)
  • ☐ Pain control (morphine/fentanyl)

📈 Normal ECG Values & Intervals

Intervals & Durations

Heart rate: 60–100 bpm (sinus)

PR interval: 120–200 ms (3–5 small boxes)

QRS duration: <120 ms (<3 small boxes)

QTc: <440 ms (men), <460 ms (women)

Axial deviation: -30° to +90° (normal axis)

ST elevation (normal): <1 mm (all leads except V2-V3)

ST elevation (V2-V3 normal): <2.5 mm (men <40), <2 mm (men >40), <1.5 mm (women)

Rate Calculation Methods

300 method: Count large boxes between R-R; 300 ÷ #boxes

1500 method: Count small boxes between R-R; 1500 ÷ #boxes (more precise)

6-second strip: Count QRS complexes × 10 (irregular rhythms)

Axis Quick Check

Lead I +/aVF +: Normal axis

Lead I +/aVF -: Left axis deviation (consider LAFB, LVH, inferior MI)

Lead I -/aVF +: Right axis deviation (consider RAD, PE, RVH)

Lead I -/aVF -: Extreme axis (VT, hyperkalemia)

⚠️ ECG Patterns That Kill — Don't Miss These

STEMI

New ST ≥1 mm (2 mm in V2-V3 men, 1.5 mm women) in 2+ contiguous leads

Action → Cath lab within 90 min

Hyperkalemia

Peaked T → wide QRS → sine wave. K⁺ >6.5

Action → CaCl₂ + insulin/glucose + albuterol + kayexalate

Torsades de Pointes

Polymorphic VT with prolonged QTc. Twisting QRS axis

Action → MgSO₄ 2g IV, overdrive pacing, stop QT-prolonging drugs

Complete Heart Block

AV dissociation: P and QRS march independently. Wide escape rhythm

Action → Atropine → transcutaneous pacing → transvenous pacer

Wellens Syndrome

Deep symmetric or biphasic T waves in V2-V3. Critical proximal LAD stenosis

Action → Urgent cath (NOT a stress test — can cause MI)

Brugada Pattern

Coved ST elevation in V1-V2 (≥2 mm) with negative T. Type I is diagnostic

Action → ICD, avoid fever, avoid Na⁺ channel blockers

⚗️ Electrolyte Management in Cardiac Patients

Potassium (K⁺) Management

Goal range: 4.0–5.0 mEq/L (higher if on digoxin)

Hypokalemia (<3.5): IV KCl 20 mEq in 100 mL D5W over 1 h; oral KCl 40–80 mEq/day

Hyperkalemia (>5.5): CaCl₂ 1g IV (stabilizes myocardium); insulin/glucose (10U + 50g dextrose); albuterol neb; kayexalate 15–30g PO/PR

ECG changes: <3.5: U waves, flat T; >6.0: peaked T, wide QRS, sine wave

Replace K⁺ & Mg²⁺ together; avoid rapid IV push in renal failure

Magnesium (Mg²⁺) Management

Goal range: 2.0–2.5 mg/dL (ionized Mg²⁺ 1.0–1.2)

Hypomagnesemia (<1.7): MgSO₄ 2g IV over 10 min, then 1g/hr drip; oral Mg oxide 400–800 mg/day

Hyperphosphatemia: Rare in cardiac patients; treat with phosphate binders if needed

Clinical pearls: Mg²⁺ essential for torsades; replace in all K⁺-depleted states; avoid in renal failure

Mg²⁺ prolongs QTc but prevents torsades; monitor for bradycardia

Calcium & Phosphate in Cardiac Care

Calcium (ionized 1.0–1.2 mmol/L): CaCl₂ 1g IV for hyperkalemia; avoid in digoxin toxicity

Phosphate (2.5–4.5 mg/dL): Replace if <2.0; K-Phos 15 mmol IV over 2 h

Monitoring: Daily BMP in ICU; adjust for renal function

Drug interactions: Loop diuretics cause K⁺/Mg²⁺ loss; ACE/ARB preserve K⁺

📐 QTc Correction Formulas & Drug-Induced QT Prolongation

QTc Formulas

Bazett's (most common): QTc = QT ÷ √RR

Fastest to calculate; overestimates at high HR, underestimates at low HR

Danger level:

Moderate: QTc 450–499 ms (men), 460–499 ms (women)

Severe/stop drug: QTc ≥500 ms or ΔQTc >60 ms from baseline

Major QT-Prolonging CV Drugs

Very high risk: Amiodarone, Sotalol, Dofetilide, Ibutilide

High risk: Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide

Moderate risk: Diltiazem, Verapamil, Flecainide

Other classes: Fluoroquinolones, Macrolides, Antipsychotics, Ondansetron, Methadone

Risk ↑ with: hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, structural heart disease, female sex, age >65

Echocardiography Interpretation Guide

POCUS cardiac views, normal measurements, and key findings for acute cardiac conditions. Based on ASE guidelines.

🔍 POCUS Cardiac Views & Landmarks

Parasternal Long Axis (PLAX)

  • • Probe: PS mid-clavicular, marker to left
  • • Structures: LV, LA, AV, MV, AoV
  • • Key: LV size/function, AV stenosis/regurg

Parasternal Short Axis (PSAX)

  • • Probe: Rotate 90° from PLAX
  • • Levels: Aortic valve, MV, Papillary muscles
  • • Key: RV size, septal motion, tamponade

Apical 4-Chamber (A4C)

  • • Probe: Apex, marker to left axilla
  • • Structures: LV, RV, LA, RA, MV, TV
  • • Key: EF, RV/LV ratio, tamponade, clots

Apical 2-Chamber (A2C)

  • • Probe: From A4C, rotate laterally
  • • Structures: LV apex/anterior, LA appendage
  • • Key: LV wall motion, thrombus

Apical 3-Chamber (A3C)

  • • Probe: From A4C, rotate medially
  • • Structures: LVOT, AV, LA, posterior wall
  • • Key: LVOT obstruction, AR severity

Subcostal 4-Chamber

  • • Probe: Below xiphoid, marker to left
  • • Structures: Similar to A4C, IVC
  • • Key: IVC collapse (preload), tamponade

🚨 Key Findings in Acute Cardiac Conditions

Cardiac Tamponade

  • ☐ RV diastolic collapse (PLAX/PSAX)
  • ☐ RA systolic collapse (A4C)
  • ☐ IVC dilated, no respiratory variation
  • ☐ Pericardial effusion + RA/RV compression
  • ☐ Pulsus paradoxus on exam
  • Action: Pericardiocentesis (subcostal approach)

Acute Severe MR

  • ☐ Flail leaflet or ruptured chordae
  • ☐ Eccentric jet toward LA wall
  • ☐ LA/LV enlargement
  • ☐ Low EF, pulmonary hypertension signs
  • Action: Afterload reduction, consider surgery

Acute LV Failure

  • ☐ LV EF <40%, global hypokinesis
  • ☐ LV dilation, spherical shape
  • ☐ Mitral regurgitation (functional)
  • ☐ B-lines on lung ultrasound
  • Action: NTG, inotropes, consider MCS

RV Strain (PE)

  • ☐ RV:LV ratio >1.0 in A4C
  • ☐ RV free wall hypokinesis
  • ☐ Septal bowing toward LV
  • ☐ TAPSE <1.7 cm
  • ☐ IVC dilated, no collapse
  • Action: Anticoagulation, consider thrombolysis

Cardiomyopathies

  • HCM: Asymmetric septal hypertrophy, SAM, LVOT obstruction
  • DCM: Dilated LV/RV, EF <40%, global hypokinesis
  • RCM: Small ventricles, thick walls, biatrial enlargement
  • ARVC: RV dilation, fatty infiltration, arrhythmias

Valvular Emergencies

  • AS: Thickened calcified AV, LVH, low gradient if low EF
  • IE: Vegetation on valve, abscess, new regurgitation
  • Aortic dissection: Intimal flap in aorta, pericardial effusion

🌊 Doppler Assessment Quick Guide

Valvular Regurgitation Grading

Mild: Narrow jet <25% LA area, vena contracta <3 mm

Moderate: Jet 25–65% LA area, vena contracta 3–7 mm

Severe: Jet >65% LA area, vena contracta >7 mm, PISA radius >9 mm

Also assess: LA size, pulmonary vein flow, LV function

Valvular Stenosis Grading

AS: Mild <2.0 cm², Moderate 1.0–1.5 cm², Severe <1.0 cm²

MS: Mild >1.5 cm², Moderate 1.0–1.5 cm², Severe <1.0 cm²

Mean gradient: AS >40 mmHg = severe

Continuity equation for AS area; pressure half-time for MS

Diastolic Function Assessment

Normal: E/A >1, DT 160–200 ms, IVRT <70 ms

Grade I (Impaired relaxation): E/A <1, DT >200 ms

Grade II (Pseudonormal): E/A 1–2, variable with Valsalva

Grade III (Restrictive): E/A >2, DT <160 ms

E/e' ratio: <8 = normal, 9–12 = indeterminate, >13 = elevated LVFP

Always correlate with BNP and clinical picture

⚠️ Disclaimer: Echo interpretation requires formal training and clinical correlation. POCUS is limited compared to comprehensive echocardiography. Always consult cardiology for definitive diagnosis. Measurements may vary with body size and age.

Pacemaker & ICD Programming Guide

Device types, programming parameters, troubleshooting, and indications. Based on ACC/AHA/HRS 2023 guidelines.

🫀 Device Types & Indications

Device Type Code Primary Indications Key Features
Single Chamber Pacemaker VVI / AAI AFib with slow ventricular response, sinus node disease One lead (RV or RA), rate response, basic pacing
Dual Chamber Pacemaker DDD AV block, sinus node disease with intact AV conduction RA + RV leads, AV synchrony, rate response
Biventricular Pacemaker CRT-P HFrEF with LBBB, NYHA II-IV despite optimal meds LV lead + RA/RV, resynchronizes ventricles
Single Chamber ICD VVI-ICD VT/VF prevention, no pacing indication RV lead only, defibrillation + pacing backup
Dual Chamber ICD DDD-ICD VT/VF prevention with pacing needs RA + RV leads, dual-chamber pacing + defibrillation
CRT-D CRT-D HFrEF + LBBB + high SCD risk LV + RA/RV leads, resynchronization + defibrillation

NBG Code: Position 1 = Chamber paced, Position 2 = Chamber sensed, Position 3 = Response to sensing, Position 4 = Programmability/Rate modulation, Position 5 = Multisite pacing

⚙️ Key Programming Parameters

Pacemaker Parameters

Base Rate:

Typically 60 bpm; can be lower (50–55) in young patients

AV Delay:

DDD: 120–200 ms (longer if PR normal); optimize for diastolic filling

Pacing Threshold:

RA: 0.4–1.0 V, RV: 0.4–1.2 V; test at implant and follow-up

Sensitivity:

RA: 0.2–0.4 mV, RV: 2.0–5.0 mV; avoid oversensing

Rate Response:

Enable for chronotropic incompetence; AV delay shortens with exercise

ICD Parameters

VF Zone:

≥220 bpm; first shock 25–35 J, subsequent higher

VT Zone:

170–200 bpm; ATP preferred, then shock if fails

SVT Discrimination:

Onset, stability, AV association algorithms to avoid inappropriate shocks

ATP (Antitachycardia Pacing):

Burst or ramp pacing to terminate VT; effective in 80–90% of cases

Detection Delay:

2.5–30 s for VT; 5–10 s for VF; balances speed vs false positives

🔄 CRT Optimization & Troubleshooting

AV Delay Optimization

Echo-guided: Maximize LVOT VTI or mitral inflow velocity

Ritter method: AV delay = 120 ms + (QRS duration - 120 ms) ÷ 2

SmartDelay™: Device algorithm adjusts AV delay automatically

Goal: AV delay allowing full ventricular filling without truncation

VV Delay Optimization

Standard: Start at 0 ms (simultaneous); adjust by 20 ms increments

Echo-guided: Maximize aortic VTI or minimize MR jet

LV first: 20–40 ms in LBBB; RV first rare

Goal: Improve septal motion and LV function

CRT Non-Responders (30% of patients)

Technical issues: Poor lead position, high threshold, diaphragmatic stimulation

Patient factors: AFib, narrow QRS, ischemic cardiomyopathy

Solutions: LV lead repositioning, AV/VV optimization, upgrade to CRT-D

Response criteria: >5% increase in EF, >10% decrease in LVESD

🔧 Common Issues & Troubleshooting

Pacemaker Problems

Failure to Capture:

Rhythm strip shows pacing spike without QRS; ↑ output to 5V, check lead impedance

Failure to Sense:

Pacing on T-wave or R-wave; ↓ sensitivity, check lead position

Diaphragmatic Pacing:

Patient hiccups with pacing; reposition lead, ↑ threshold acceptance

Pacemaker Syndrome:

Fatigue, hypotension with VVI pacing; upgrade to DDD

ICD Problems

Inappropriate Shocks:

Check for AFib, SVT, lead fracture, T-wave oversensing; reprogram zones

Failure to Deliver Shock:

High DFT; reposition lead, add SVC coil, consider subcutaneous ICD

Multiple Therapies:

Short detection delay or redetection after ATP; ↑ delay, disable redetection

Post-Shock Bradycardia:

Common after VF storm; ensure backup pacing is enabled

📊 Follow-Up & Remote Monitoring

Routine Follow-Up Schedule

Post-implant: 1–2 weeks (wound check, parameters)

Early: 3 months (optimize settings, check thresholds)

Routine: Every 3–6 months (battery, leads, function)

Annual: Comprehensive eval with echo if CRT

Remote Monitoring

Benefits: Early detection of issues, reduced clinic visits

Data transmitted: Arrhythmias, impedance, battery status

Alerts: Lead issues, low battery, shocks, AFib burden

Frequency: Daily/weekly depending on device and patient risk

Pediatric Cardiology Reference

Congenital heart disease overview, pediatric-specific algorithms, and age-adjusted dosing. Based on ACC/AHA 2022 CHD guidelines.

🫀 Common Congenital Heart Diseases

Left-to-Right Shunts

  • VSD: Most common CHD (30–40%); small close spontaneously
  • ASD: Secundum type (80%); PFO common in infants
  • PDA: Common in preterm; closes with indomethacin
  • AV Canal: Associated with Down syndrome

Right-to-Left Shunts (Cyanotic)

  • Tetralogy of Fallot: Boot-shaped heart, RVOT obstruction
  • Transposition of Great Arteries: Parallel circulations; duct-dependent
  • Tricuspid Atresia: Hypoplastic RV, ASD required
  • Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return: All pulmonary veins drain anomalously

Obstructive Lesions

  • Coarctation of Aorta: Hypertension in upper extremities
  • Aortic Stenosis: Bicuspid valve common; may progress
  • Pulmonary Stenosis: Often mild; balloon valvuloplasty if severe
  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy: Genetic; beta-blockers first-line

Other Common Defects

  • Patent Foramen Ovale: Probe-patent; usually closes by 1 year
  • Ventricular Septal Defect: Muscular vs membranous; Eisenmenger if untreated
  • Atrioventricular Septal Defect: Complete vs partial; Rastelli classification
  • Double Outlet Right Ventricle: Both great arteries from RV

Single Ventricle Physiology

  • Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome: Norwood procedure
  • Tricuspid Atresia: Fontan palliation
  • Double Inlet Left Ventricle: Variable presentation
  • Fontan Circulation: Passive pulmonary blood flow

Associated Syndromes

  • Down Syndrome: AVSD, ASD, VSD common
  • Turner Syndrome: Coarctation, bicuspid aortic valve
  • Williams Syndrome: Supravalvar AS, peripheral PS
  • 22q11 Deletion: TOF, interrupted aortic arch

🚨 Pediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS)

Cardiac Arrest Algorithm

CPR: 15:2 ratio (infant/child); 30:2 (adolescent)

Compression depth: 1/3 AP diameter (~4 cm infant, 5 cm child)

Epinephrine: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO q3–5 min (1:10,000)

Defibrillation: 2 J/kg initial, then 4 J/kg

Airway: BVM preferred initially; advanced airway when skilled

Bradycardia Algorithm

Assess: HR <60 bpm with poor perfusion

Support ABCs: Oxygen, ventilation, chest compressions

Epinephrine: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO q3–5 min

Atropine: 0.02 mg/kg IV (min 0.1 mg, max 0.5 mg)

Pacing: Consider if unresponsive to drugs

Tachycardia Algorithm

Stable SVT: Vagal maneuvers → adenosine 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 6 mg)

Unstable SVT: Synchronized cardioversion 0.5–1 J/kg

Stable VT: Amiodarone 5 mg/kg IV over 20–60 min

Unstable VT: Defibrillate 2 J/kg

Consider toxins: TCA overdose, digoxin toxicity

Post-Resuscitation Care

Therapeutic hypothermia: Consider 32–34°C × 24–48 h

Targeted glucose: 80–110 mg/dL

Neurologic monitoring: EEG if indicated

Echo: Assess cardiac function post-arrest

⚠️ Common Pediatric Cardiac Emergencies

Duct-Dependent Lesions

Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1):

Dose: 0.05–0.1 mcg/kg/min IV; maintains PDA patency

Indications: HLHS, critical AS/PS, TGA with intact septum

Side effects: Apnea, fever, hypotension

Balloon Atrial Septostomy:

Emergency procedure for TGA; creates ASD for mixing

Kawasaki Disease

Criteria (≥4 of 5):

Fever ≥5 days, conjunctivitis, rash, cervical LAD, extremity changes

Treatment:

IVIG 2 g/kg + aspirin 80–100 mg/kg/day divided QID

Echo at diagnosis and 2 weeks; consider steroids if refractory

Myocarditis

Presentation: Fever, chest pain, fatigue, arrhythmias

Workup: Troponin, BNP, echo, viral studies

Management: Supportive care, antiarrhythmics if needed

Prognosis: Most recover; consider transplant if severe

Cardiac Transplant

Immunosuppression: Tacrolimus/cyclosporine + mycophenolate + steroids

Rejection monitoring: Echo, BNP, endomyocardial biopsy

Infections: CMV prophylaxis; monitor for PTLD

Long-term: Coronary vasculopathy screening

🔄 Fontan Physiology & Complications

Physiology

Circuit: SVC/IVC → pulmonary arteries → lungs → LA → LV

Driving pressure: Mean venous pressure (normal 10–15 mmHg)

Cardiac output: Dependent on preload; no subpulmonary ventricle

Oxygenation: Usually normal at rest; desaturates with exertion

Complications

Plastic bronchitis: Cast formation in airways; sirolimus treatment

Protein-losing enteropathy: Hypoproteinemia; treat with diuretics/budenoside

Fontan-associated liver disease: Cirrhosis; monitor LFTs

Thromboembolism: Anticoagulation with warfarin or DOAC

Arrhythmias: Atrial flutter common; ablation or antiarrhythmics

Fontan Failure

Signs: Exercise intolerance, edema, ascites, pleural effusions

Workup: Echo, cath, BNP, liver imaging

Management: Diuretics, anticoagulation, rate control

Advanced: Heart transplant or mechanical support
