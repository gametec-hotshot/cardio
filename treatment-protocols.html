Heart Failure Treatment Protocol

ACC/AHA 2022 Guidelines

HFrEF (EF ≤ 40%) Treatment Algorithm

1

First-Line Therapy

Class I Recommendation
ACE Inhibitors
  • • Enalapril: 2.5-20mg BID
  • • Ramipril: 1.25-10mg daily
  • • Lisinopril: 5-40mg daily
  • • Target: Max tolerated evidence-based dose
Beta-Blockers
  • • Carvedilol: 3.125-25mg BID
  • • Metoprolol succinate: 12.5-200mg daily
  • • Bisoprolol: 1.25-10mg daily
  • • Titrate to max tolerated dose
Clinical Pearls
  • • Start ACE inhibitor first, then add beta-blocker once stable
  • • Titrate both medications to maximum tolerated evidence-based doses
  • • Monitor renal function and electrolytes with each dose change
  • • Continue diuretics as needed for symptom control
2

Second-Line Therapy

Class I Recommendation
MRA
  • • Spironolactone: 25mg daily
  • • Eplerenone: 25mg daily
  • • Monitor K+ and creatinine
SGLT2 Inhibitors
  • • Dapagliflozin: 10mg daily
  • • Empagliflozin: 10mg daily
  • • Reduces CV death and HF hospitalization
ARNI Alternative
  • • Sacubitril/valsartan: 24/26mg BID
  • • Superior to ACE inhibitors
  • • 36-hour washout required
3

Third-Line/Specialist Referral

Class IIa Recommendation
Ivabradine
  • • Starting: 5mg BID
  • • Target: 7.5mg BID
  • • HR ≥75 bpm, sinus rhythm
  • • Reduces CV death and HF hospitalization
Device Therapy
  • • ICD if EF ≤35% and NYHA II-III
  • • CRT if QRS ≥150ms and LBBB
  • • Consider cardiac transplantation

HFpEF (EF ≥ 50%) Treatment

Pharmacologic Management

  • Diuretics: For volume management and symptom relief
  • SGLT2 Inhibitors: Dapagliflozin or empagliflozin reduce HF hospitalizations
  • ARB/ARNI: Consider for hypertension management

Comorbidity Management

  • Hypertension: Target <130/80 mmHg
  • Diabetes: Optimize glycemic control
  • Atrial Fibrillation: Rate and rhythm control

Medical Management

  • • Aspirin 81mg daily (lifelong)
  • • Clopidogrel 75mg daily if aspirin contraindicated
  • • Dual antiplatelet therapy post-PCI/stent

Statin Therapy

  • • High-intensity statin for all patients
  • • Atorvastatin 40-80mg daily
  • • Rosuvastatin 20-40mg daily
  • • Target LDL-C <70 mg/dL

  • • Metoprolol succinate 25-200mg daily
  • • Carvedilol 3.125-25mg BID
  • • Titrate to resting HR 55-60 bpm

Revascularization Strategy

PCI (Percutaneous Coronary Intervention)

  • • Primary PCI for STEMI within 90 minutes
  • • Elective PCI for high-risk anatomy
  • • Drug-eluting stents preferred
  • • Dual antiplatelet therapy 6-12 months

CABG (Coronary Artery Bypass Graft)

  • • Left main disease (>50% stenosis)
  • • Three-vessel disease with diabetes
  • • Two-vessel disease with proximal LAD
  • • SYNTAX score >22

Step 1 — Diagnostic Classification

STEMI

ST-Elevation MI

ECG Diagnostic Criteria
  • • ST elevation: ≥2 mm in V2–V3 (men), ≥1.5 mm in V2–V3 (women), ≥1 mm in all other contiguous leads
  • • New LBBB: with ischemic symptoms (consider Sgarbossa criteria)
  • • Posterior MI: ST depression V1–V3 + tall R waves → place posterior leads V7–V9
  • • de Winter T waves: Upsloping ST depression V1–V6 + tall symmetric T waves → proximal LAD occlusion
  • • Wellens syndrome: Deeply inverted or biphasic T waves in V2–V3 → critical LAD stenosis
  • • Right ventricular MI: Inferior STEMI + ST elevation V4R → avoid nitro/diuretics

🚨 Do NOT wait for troponin — activate cath lab on ECG alone

NSTE-ACS

NSTEMI & Unstable Angina

Diagnostic Criteria
NSTEMI
  • • Ischemic symptoms
  • • ST depression ≥0.5 mm
  • • T-wave inversion
  • • ↑Troponin (above 99th percentile URL)
Unstable Angina
  • • Ischemic symptoms
  • • ST/T changes or none
  • • Normal troponin
  • • New onset, rest, or crescendo angina

⚡ Risk-stratify with TIMI or GRACE score

Very high risk → immediate cath (<2h). High risk → early (<24h). Intermediate → within 72h.

Beta-Blockers

  • • Carvedilol: 3.125–25 mg PO BID
  • • Bisoprolol: 1.25–10 mg PO daily

⚠️ IV βB contraindicated in STEMI with shock risk factors (age >70, HR >110, SBP <120) — COMMIT trial showed ↑cardiogenic shock and mortality

A
ACE Inhibitors / ARBs
  • • Lisinopril: 2.5–10 mg PO daily (start within 24h if EF <40%, anterior MI, or HF)
  • • Enalapril: 2.5–20 mg PO daily
  • • Valsartan: 80–160 mg BID (if ACEi intolerant)

✓ SAVE/AIRE/TRACE: ACEi reduces re-infarction and mortality post-MI

S
Statin (High-Intensity)
  • • Atorvastatin: 80 mg PO daily
  • • Rosuvastatin: 40 mg PO daily
  • • Target LDL-C <70 mg/dL (<55 mg/dL very high risk, ESC)

✓ PROVE-IT: High-intensity statin 16% RRR in MACE vs. moderate

H — Heparin (Anticoagulation)

STEMI (PCI)
  • • UFH: 70–100 U/kg IV bolus (if no GPI), 50–70 U/kg (with GPI)
  • • Bivalirudin: 0.75 mg/kg IV bolus → 1.75 mg/kg/hr infusion
  • • Enoxaparin: 0.5 mg/kg IV bolus (PCI-compatible dose)
NSTEMI/UA
  • • Enoxaparin: 1 mg/kg SC BID (preferred, ESSENCE/TIMI 11B)
  • • Fondaparinux: 2.5 mg SC daily (OASIS-5: less bleeding, but add UFH during PCI)
  • • UFH: 60 U/kg IV bolus (max 4000 U) → 12 U/kg/hr (max 1000 U/hr), target aPTT 1.5–2.5×

Step 3 — Reperfusion Strategy

FIRST-LINE

Primary PCI

Preferred if first medical contact (FMC) to device ≤120 minutes

Time Targets
  • • Door-to-balloon: <90 min (PCI-capable center)
  • • Door-to-balloon: <120 min (after transfer to PCI center)
  • • FMC-to-device: <120 min (including transport)
Radial vs. Femoral Access

Radial preferred: MATRIX trial — ↓access site bleeding (44% ↓ major bleeding), ↓all-cause mortality vs. femoral

Multi-vessel Disease
  • CULPRIT-SHOCK (NEJM 2018): Culprit-lesion-only PCI at index procedure preferred in STEMI-CS. ↓30-day mortality (45.9% vs. 55.4%, OR 0.67)
  • COMPLETE (NEJM 2019): In STEMI without shock with multivessel CAD, staged complete revascularization ↓ composite CV death/new MI by 26% (HR 0.74). Index or staged (~45 days) both acceptable.
ALTERNATIVE

Fibrinolytic Therapy

If PCI not accessible within 120 min of FMC

Agents & Dosing
  • Tenecteplase (TNK-tPA): Single IV weight-based bolus (30–50 mg over 5–10 sec). Preferred agent.
  • Alteplase (rtPA): 15 mg IV bolus → 0.75 mg/kg ×30 min (max 50 mg) → 0.5 mg/kg ×60 min (max 35 mg). Total max 100 mg.
  • Door-to-needle target: <30 min
Pharmaco-invasive Strategy

STREAM (NEJM 2013): If fibrinolysis is given → routine angiography at 6–24h (regardless of clinical response). Rescue PCI if failed fibrinolysis (≤50% ST resolution at 60–90 min).

Absolute Contraindications
  • ❌ Prior intracranial hemorrhage / stroke of any kind
  • ❌ Known cerebral vascular lesion or malignant intracranial neoplasm
  • ❌ Ischemic stroke within 3 months (EXCEPT <3h acute stroke)
  • ❌ Suspected aortic dissection
  • ❌ Active bleeding or bleeding diathesis (excl. menses)
  • ❌ Significant closed-head/facial trauma within 3 months

Key relative: Severe uncontrolled HTN (SBP >180/DBP >110), recent major surgery (<3 weeks), current anticoagulant use, pregnancy, noncompressible vascular puncture.

⏱️ DAPT Duration After ACS

Standard Course

  • • 12 months DAPT (ASA + preferred P2Y12) for all ACS patients
  • • After 12 months: Aspirin 81 mg daily indefinitely
  • • High bleeding risk: 3–6 months then P2Y12 monotherapy
  • • TWILIGHT (NEJM 2019): After 3 months DAPT post-PCI, drop aspirin → ticagrelor monotherapy ↓ bleeding (HR 0.56) without ↑ischemic events

Extended DAPT / Special Scenarios

  • • High ischemic risk: Extend DAPT beyond 12 months — PEGASUS-TIMI 54: Ticagrelor 60 mg BID ↓ MACE 15% (HR 0.85) but ↑major bleeding
  • • Concomitant AFib: Triple therapy (OAC + ASA + clopidogrel) ≤1 month, then OAC + clopidogrel to 12 months (AUGUSTUS). Avoid prasugrel/ticagrelor with triple therapy.
  • • Post-CABG: Add clopidogrel 75 mg daily ×12 months to aspirin (CURE subgroup)

Step 6 — Complication Recognition & Management

⚡ Cardiogenic Shock

  • • Emergent cath + culprit PCI — SHOCK Trial: ↓mortality 18% at 6 months
  • • CULPRIT-lesion first — defer non-culprit PCI (CULPRIT-SHOCK)
  • • Consider MCS: Impella (LV unloading), VA-ECMO (refractory). IABP NOT routine (IABP-SHOCK II)
  • • Avoid routine IABP — no mortality benefit unless mechanical complication

⚡ Mechanical Complications

  • • VSD: Emergent surgical repair. IABP as bridge. Typically occurs 3–5 days post-MI
  • • Free wall rupture: Immediate surgery. IABP + pericardiocentesis as temporizing. Usually fatal
  • • Papillary muscle rupture → acute MR: IABP + emergent surgery. Medical bridge: nitroprusside (reduces afterload)

⚡ Arrhythmias

  • • VF/pVT (primary): Defibrillation → ACLS. Does NOT warrant ICD unless persistent/recurring (>48h)
  • • Complete heart block (inferior MI): Atropine → temporary pacing if refractory. Usually resolves
  • • AFib post-MI: Rate control (βB) + anticoagulation if not in sinus rhythm within 48h

⚡ Pericarditis / Dressler Syndrome

  • • Early post-MI pericarditis (1–3 days): High-dose aspirin 650 mg q4–6h. Avoid NSAIDs/steroids — impair scar formation, risk of rupture
  • • Dressler syndrome (weeks–months): Aspirin or NSAIDs ± colchicine 0.6 mg BID
  • • Pericardial effusion: Serial echo. Tamponade → emergent pericardiocentesis

Step 7 — Secondary Prevention (The "5 As + Lipids")

A

Antiplatelet Therapy

Aspirin 81 mg daily + P2Y12 inhibitor ×12 months (or per individualized strategy above). Lifelong aspirin after DAPT completion.

A

ACE Inhibitor / ARB

Indefinitely if LVEF <40%, HTN, DM, or CKD (SAVE, AIRE, TRACE). Target BP <130/80 mmHg.

A

Aldosterone Antagonist

Eplerenone 25–50 mg daily if LVEF ≤40% + HF symptoms or DM post-MI (EPHESUS). Monitor K+ and creatinine.

B

Beta-Blocker

Continue ≥3 years post-MI. Indefinite if LVEF <40%. Titrate to HR 55–60 bpm. Carvedilol/metoprolol succinate/bisoprolol.

L

Lipid Management

High-intensity statin indefinitely. Add ezetimibe if LDL-C ≥70 mg/dL on max statin (IMPROVE-IT: 6% RRR). Add PCSK9i if needed (FOURIER/ODYSSEY: 15% MACE ↓). Target LDL-C <55 mg/dL for post-MI very high risk.

C

Cardiac Rehab + Cessation

Enroll in cardiac rehabilitation program (↓20–30% in CV mortality). Smoking cessation (varenicline > bupropion > NRT). Mediterranean diet. Exercise: ≥150 min/week moderate aerobic. Weight management.

Class I
Anticoagulation (Hemodynamically Stable)
  • • Same DOAC regimens as DVT

⚠️ High-Risk (Massive) PE — Emergency

SBP <90 mmHg for ≥15 min, or requiring vasopressors, or cardiac arrest.

  • • Systemic thrombolysis: alteplase 100mg IV over 2h, then heparin infusion
  • • If thrombolysis contraindicated or failed → surgical embolectomy or catheter-directed therapy
  • • ICU admission, vasopressor support as needed

Special Populations

CHEST / ESC

Cancer-Associated VTE

  • • Preferred: Apixaban or rivaroxaban (or LMWH if GI/GU risk)
  • • DOAC preferred over LMWH if no high bleeding-risk GI or GU primary tumor
  • • Minimum duration: 6 months; extend if active cancer
  • • IVC filter only if contraindication to anticoagulation

  • Reference: Key et al., CHEST 2018, 154(5):1034–1052 (ASCO 2019 update)

Pregnancy-Associated VTE

  • • Preferred: LMWH (not DOACs) throughout pregnancy
  • • Switch to UFH close to expected delivery
  • • Continue postpartum anticoagulation for ≥6 weeks (minimum 3 months total)
  • • Warfarin contraindicated in 1st trimester (teratogenic)

  • Reference: Bates et al., CHEST 2018, 154(5):1126–1183 (ACCP Guidelines)

Renal Impairment

  • • CrCl <30 mL/min: Avoid DOACs (limited data); use LMWH or UFH
  • • CrCl 15-29 mL/min: LMWH with dose adjustment (anti-Xa monitoring)
  • • On hemodialysis: consider UFH; apixaban may be used (limited data)
  • • Consider dose-reduced apixaban or rivaroxaban if CrCl 15-29 (off-label)

  • Reference: Stevens et al., CHEST 2018, 154(5):1159–1174

IVC Filter Indications

  • • Contraindication to anticoagulation (acute bleeding)
  • • Recurrent VTE despite adequate anticoagulation
  • • Retrieve filter as soon as anticoagulation is safe
  • • NOT recommended as primary treatment over anticoagulation

  • Reference: Kearon et al., CHEST 2016, 149(1):31–50 (CHEST 2021 Update)

Cardiogenic Shock Treatment Protocol

SCAI 2024 / AHA 2023 Updates

🚨 Definition — SCAI 2019 Criteria

Cardiogenic shock (CS) is characterized by end-organ hypoperfusion due to cardiac dysfunction. Diagnosis requires all three:

Hemodynamics

  • • SBP <90 mmHg OR MAP <65
  • • CI <2.2 L/min/m²
  • • RA pressure >15 mmHg OR PA wedge >15

Hypoperfusion (≥1)

  • • Altered mentation
  • • Cool, clammy extremities
  • • Urine output <30 mL/hr
  • • Lactate >2 mmol/L

Cardiac Etiology

  • • Acute MI (~70% of cases)
  • • Decompensated heart failure
  • • Fulminant myocarditis
  • • Mechanical complications
  • • Stress cardiomyopathy

SCAI Shock Staging Classification (A → E)

Stage Category Key Features Management Goal
A Not in Shock At risk for CS but normotensive, no hypoperfusion Prevent progression; optimize HF therapy
B Beginning Shock SBP <90 but no hypoperfusion; or hypotensive only
responds to fluid. No shock index (HR/SBP) >0.9		 Early recognition; vasopressors if fluid-refractory; rule out reversible causes
C Classic Shock Hypotension + hypoperfusion; needs vasoactive drugs to maintain BP/CI; lactate usually elevated Full CS bundle — vasoactive drugs, consider MCS, urgent cath if MI
D Deteriorating Clinical deterioration despite therapy; escalating doses of inotropes/pressors; worsening renal function or lactate rise Upscale to MCS if not already; consider VA-ECMO; transfer to advanced center
E Extremis / Collapse Cardiopulmonary arrest; profound shock with imminent death; CPR or VA-ECMO required for survival E-CPR with VA-ECMO; emergent PCI if AMI; transplant/LVAD evaluation
💡 Key Concept: The "Shock Spiral"

Cardiogenic shock is a vicious cycle: ↓CO → ↓coronary perfusion → ↓contractility → ↓CO. Early aggressive management breaks the cycle. Every hour of delay in revascularization increases mortality by 12.5% (SHOCK Trial). Staging drives urgency — Stage C/D/E require immediate escalation.

🔬 Diagnostic Workup (SIMULTANEOUS with treatment)

  • ☐ 12-lead ECG — STEMI → cath lab NOW
  • ☐ Troponin, BNP, lactate, ABG, CBC, BMP, LFTs, coags
  • ☐ Bedside echo (POCUS) — EF, valvular, tamponade, RV function
  • ☐ Right heart cath (Swan-Ganz) — CI, SVR, wedge pressure if uncertain
  • ☐ CXR — pulmonary edema, pneumothorax, device check
  • ☐ Foley catheter — serial urine output (target ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • ☐ Arterial line — continuous BP monitoring

🫁 Supportive Care

  • ☐ Oxygen: SpO₂ target 92–96%, intubate if GCS ↓ or respiratory failure
  • ☐ Intubation caution: Positive pressure can worsen RV failure; hemodynamic collapse on induction is common. Use ketamine or etomidate. Have push-dose pressor ready.
  • ☐ IV access: 2 large-bore peripheral lines or central venous catheter
  • ☐ Fluid challenge: 250–500 mL crystalloid ONLY if no obvious volume overload or echo suggests RV failure
  • ☐ Diuretics only IF pulmonary edema WITHOUT shock (rare in CS)

Vasoactive Agent Selection & Dosing

1

First-Line Vasoactive Agents

Start Here → Titrate to MAP ≥65
Norepinephrine (Levophed) — Vasopressor of Choice
Dose:
0.01–0.5 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
Receptor:
α₁ > β₁ (vasoconstriction + modest inotropy)

Why first-line: Sepsis in CS (SOAP II), NE associated with lower mortality than dopamine in CS. Increases MAP without excessive tachycardia.

Target: MAP ≥65 mmHg (may need ≥70 in chronic HTN). Titrate q5–15 min.

Dobutamine — Inotrope of Choice
Dose:
2–20 mcg/kg/min IV infusion
Receptor:
β₁ > β₂ (positive inotropy + vasodilation)

When to use: Low CO (CI <2.2) despite adequate MAP. Often combined with NE (NE for BP, dobutamine for CO). Start dobutamine only after MAP stabilized with pressor.

Caution: Can cause vasodilation → hypotension. May increase HR and myocardial O₂ demand. Tachyarrhythmia risk.

Milrinone — PDE3 Inhibitor
Dose:
0.125–0.75 mcg/kg/min (no bolus in shock)
Receptor:
PDE3 inhibition → ↑cAMP (inotropy + vasodilation)

Preferred when: Patient on beta-blockers (dobutamine effect blunted), RV failure (selective pulmonary vasodilation), long-term HF bridge.

Major caution: Renally cleared — halve dose if CrCl <50. Longer half-life — harder to stop if hypotension worsens. Must combine with pressor.

Epinephrine — Rescue Agent
Dose:
0.01–0.5 mcg/kg/min IV infusion (bolus 1 mg for arrest)
Receptor:
α₁, β₁, β₂ (dose-dependent)

When to escalate: Refractory CS despite NE + dobutamine. Provides both vasopression and inotropy. Higher lactic acidosis and tachyarrhythmia risk vs NE alone.

LOW dose (<0.1): β → inotropy | HIGH dose (>0.1): α → vasoconstriction

2

Vasopressin — Second-Line Adjunct

V2 Receptor agonist
Dose: 0.01–0.04 U/min IV (fixed — do NOT titrate)
Use: Add to NE when NE dose >0.25–0.5 mcg/kg/min. Reduces NE requirement ("catecholamine sparer"). Non-adrenergic mechanism. May worsen RV afterload.

📐 How to Choose MCS
Early/Stage C CS: If persistent hypoperfusion despite drugs → Impella CP (LV support) or IABP (post-MI). Consider early — "door to unloading" in STEMI-CS may reduce mortality.
Escalation/Stage D–E: If deteriorating on Impella + pressors → VA-ECMO. For arrest or profound biventricular failure → VA-ECMO first. E-CPR if arrest >10 min.
Right Ventricular Failure: Consider TandemHeart with RV add-on (TandemHeart Right) or Impella RP. Isolated RV failure: inhaled nitric oxide, milrinone, RVAD.
Bridge to Decision: If etiology unclear or potentially reversible (myocarditis, reversible ischemia) → VA-ECMO. If chronic end-stage HF → evaluate for LVAD or heart transplant.

Etiology-Specific Management

🫀 AMI-Induced CS (~70% of cases)

  • → Emergent coronary angiography + revascularization is Class I recommendation (SHOCK Trial — 18% absolute mortality reduction at 6 months)
  • → Multi-vessel PCI: culprit-lesion-first strategy (CULPRIT-SHOCK trial — staged non-culprit PCI)
  • → DAPT: Aspirin 325 mg + ticagrelor 180 mg (if anatomy defined) or clopidogrel 600 mg if CABG likely
  • → Consider Impella before PCI ("door to unloading") to improve outcomes
  • → Avoid fibrinolytics in shock (poor efficacy, bleeding risk) — PCI is definitive

⚙️ Mechanical Complications (Post-MI)

  • → Acute MR (papillary muscle rupture): IABP + emergent surgery. Nitroprusside if stable enough to bridge.
  • → VSD: IABP + emergent surgical repair. Delay if possible (≥2 weeks for tissue integrity) but rarely possible in shock.
  • → Free wall rupture: Immediate pericardiocentesis + emergent surgery. Often fatal before intervention.
  • → LV aneurysm: Usually subacute; less commonly shock. Managed medically unless thrombus/arrhythmia.

🦠 Fulminant Myocarditis

  • → Supportive care is mainstay. Inotropes (dobutamine/milrinone) + pressors (NE)
  • → VA-ECMO for refractory cases — excellent bridge to recovery
  • → Endomyocardial biopsy if diagnosis uncertain or suspecting giant cell/eosinophilic
  • → Immunosuppression (steroids) for giant cell, eosinophilic, sarcoid — NOT for viral
  • → Fulminant myocarditis has ~80% recovery rate with aggressive support

💔 Takotsubo (Stress) Cardiomyopathy

  • → Apical ballooning on echo — mimics STEMI ACS presentation
  • → Coronary angiography shows no obstructive CAD
  • → AVOID inotropes if possible — catecholamine surge is pathophysiologic mechanism
  • → Use IABP or Impella over catecholamines for hemodynamic support
  • → Most recover within 2–8 weeks. Supportive care. Beta-blockers controversial.

Therapeutic Endpoints & Monitoring

✅ Hemodynamic Targets

  • • MAP ≥ 65 mmHg (higher, e.g., 70–75, in chronic HTN)
  • • CI ≥ 2.2 L/min/m²
  • • SvO₂ ≥ 65% (mixed venous O₂ saturation)
  • • Lactate clearance — trending down by 10% every 2 hours
  • • Urine output ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • • SVR: Target 800–1,200 dyn·s·cm⁻⁵

📊 Monitoring Tools

  • • Arterial line — continuous BP
  • • Central venous catheter — CVP, drug delivery
  • • Swan-Ganz (PAC) — CI, SVR, PA wedge (gold standard for titration)
  • • Serial lactate — q2-4h until normalization (<2 mmol/L)
  • • POCUS — IVC, LV function, RV function, pericardium, lung B-lines
  • • Cardiac biomarkers — q6h troponin (MI evolution tracking)
  • • ScvO₂ from central line — surrogate for SvO₂

⚠️ Contraindications & Inotrope Hazards

Do NOT escalate inotropes when:
  • • Hypovolemic shock — give fluids, not pressors/inotropes
  • • Obstructive shock (tamponade, massive PE, tension PTX) — fix the obstruction
  • • Takotsubo cardiomyopathy — worsens with catecholamines
  • • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy — inotropes worsen LVOT obstruction
Inotrope toxicity risk factors:
  • • Prolonged high-dose dobutamine/epinephrine → arrhythmias, increased MVO₂, myocardial necrosis
  • • Milrinone accumulation in renal failure → refractory hypotension
  • • All inotropes ↑mortality in long-term HF (OPTTIME-CHF)
  • • Key principle: Inotropes are a bridge — fix the cause (revascularize), unload the LV (MCS), or transition to destination therapy

Type A

Ascending Aorta Involved

  • ● Affects ascending aorta ± arch ± descending
  • ● ~60-70% of all dissections
  • ● Immediate surgical emergency
  • ● Highest mortality without timely intervention
Type B

Descending Aorta Only

  • ● Distal to left subclavian artery origin
  • ● ~30-40% of all dissections
  • ● Medical management if uncomplicated
  • ● TEVAR/endovascular for complicated cases

DeBakey Classification

Type I

Originates in ascending aorta, extends through arch into descending aorta

Type II

Confined to ascending aorta only

Type III

Originates in descending aorta (IIIa = above diaphragm, IIIb = below)

Predisposing Conditions & Risk Factors

Heritable Connective Tissue Disorders

  • ◆ Marfan syndrome — FBN1 mutation, fibrillin-1 deficiency. Cystic medial necrosis.
  • ◆ Loeys-Dietz syndrome — TGFBR1/2 mutations. Aggressive at smaller aortic diameters.
  • ◆ Ehlers-Danlos (vascular) — COL3A1 mutation. Fragile vessels, extreme surgical risk.
  • ◆ Turner syndrome — Bicuspid aortic valve, ascending aortopathy.

Acquired Risk Factors

  • ◆ Chronic hypertension — Present in 70-80%. Most important modifiable factor.
  • ◆ Bicuspid aortic valve — Associated aortopathy independent of valve dysfunction.
  • ◆ Cocaine/amphetamine — Catecholamine surge. Consider in young patients.
  • ◆ Atherosclerosis — Penetrating ulcer can evolve into dissection.
  • ◆ Iatrogenic — Post-catheterization, post-cardiac surgery.
  • ◆ Aortitis — Giant cell arteritis, Takayasu, syphilitic aortitis.
  • ◆ Pregnancy — Hormonal weakening of media. 3rd trimester/postpartum peak risk.

Immediate Management (First Hour)

🚨 Anti-Impulse Therapy

Goal: Reduce aortic wall shear stress (dP/dt). Target SBP 100–120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm.

Step 1: Beta-Blockade (ALWAYS FIRST)

Reduce HR BEFORE vasodilators to prevent reflex tachycardia.

Esmolol (Preferred)

• Bolus: 500 mcg/kg IV → Inf: 50–200 mcg/kg/min

Ultra-short acting, easy titration

Labetalol

• 20 mg IV q10min (max 300 mg) or infusion 0.5–2 mg/min

Alpha + beta. Avoid in asthma.

Step 2: Vasodilators (AFTER Beta-Blockade)

If SBP >120 despite adequate beta-blockade.

Nitroprusside

• Start 0.5 mcg/kg/min, max 10 mcg/kg/min

⚠️ Must have beta-blockade first!

Nicardipine

• 5 mg/hr, increase by 2.5 mg/hr q15min, max 15 mg/hr

Clevidipine

• 1–2 mg/hr, double q90sec, max 21 mg/hr

Definition & Classification

EMERGENCY

Hypertensive Emergency

  • • BP usually >180/120 mmHg
  • • With progressive target-organ damage
  • • Requires immediate BP reduction with IV agents
  • • Manage in ICU with arterial line monitoring

Target-organ manifestations:

• Encephalopathy • Intracranial hemorrhage • Acute pulmonary edema • Aortic dissection • ACS / unstable angina • Acute kidney injury • Preeclampsia / eclampsia • MAHA
URGENCY

Hypertensive Urgency

  • • BP >180/120 mmHg without acute end-organ damage
  • • NO IV therapy required
  • • Adjust or restart oral antihypertensives
  • • Outpatient management or observation unit
  • • Gradual reduction over 24–48 hours

Key distinction:

Presence of end-organ damage defines emergency vs urgency, NOT the absolute BP number. A patient at 160/100 with acute pulmonary edema IS a hypertensive emergency. A patient at 240/140 with no organ damage is hypertensive urgency.

Initial Evaluation (First 15 Minutes)

Diagnostic Tests

  • ☐ 12-lead ECG — ischemia, LVH, strain pattern
  • ☐ Troponin (hs-cTn) — myocardial injury
  • ☐ BNP / NT-proBNP — heart failure
  • ☐ CMP — creatinine, Na+, K+, BUN
  • ☐ Urinalysis — hematuria, proteinuria
  • ☐ CBC — MAHA (schistocytes, thrombocytopenia)
  • ☐ CT head non-contrast — ICH or PRES
  • ☐ Bedside echo — LV function, wall motion, effusion
  • ☐ Fundoscopy — papilledema, hemorrhages, exudates

Initial Stabilization

  • ☐ ICU admission — continuous cardiac & hemodynamic monitoring
  • ☐ Arterial line — beat-to-beat BP monitoring for IV titration
  • ☐ Confirm true BP — repeat on opposite arm, correct cuff size
  • ☐ Two large-bore IVs — central line for continuous infusion
  • ☐ Reversible causes — nonadherence, sympathomimetics, MAOI+tyramine, acute pain
  • ☐ Foley catheter — serial urine output (target ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr)

Agent Selection Algorithm

Neurological Emergencies
  • • Encephalopathy/PRES: Nicardipine or labetalol
  • • Acute ischemic stroke: Nicardipine, labetalol (permissive HTN)
  • • ICH: Nicardipine or labetalol (target SBP 140)
  • ⚠ Avoid nitroprusside (↓ICP)
Cardiac Emergencies
  • • Pulmonary edema: Nitroglycerin ± nicardipine
  • • ACS: Nitroglycerin (preload+afterload+coronary)
  • • Aortic dissection: Esmolol/labetalol FIRST → then nitroprusside/nicardipine
  • ⚠ Avoid hydralazine (worsens shear stress)
Renal & Other
  • • AKI: Fenoldopam (renal vasodilation)
  • • Preeclampsia: Labetalol, hydralazine, PO nifedipine
  • • Cocaine: Benzodiazepines first → phentolamine or nicardipine
  • ⚠ Avoid β-blocker alone in cocaine toxicity

⚠ Critical Pitfalls to Avoid

  • ⚠ Short-acting oral nifedipine (sublingual/capsule-piercing) →→ unpredictable drops, stroke, MI, death (FDA black box warning)
  • ⚠ >25% BP drop in first hour → watershed infarcts, MI, AKI
  • ⚠ Nitroprusside without β-blockade → reflex tachycardia; worsens shear stress in dissection
  • ⚠ Standalone β-blocker in cocaine toxicity → unopposed α-vasoconstriction; use BDZs first
  • ⚠ Nitroprusside in elevated ICP/ICH → cerebral vasodilation worsens ICP
  • ⚠ Enalaprilat in pregnancy or bilateral RAS → teratogenic; renal failure
  • ⚠ Hydralazine in aortic dissection → reflex tachycardia worsens propagation
  • ⚠ Nitroglycerin with PDE-5 inhibitors → profound refractory hypotension wait 24h (sildenafil) or 48h (tadalafil)

Transition to Oral Therapy

Principles

  • • Start oral agents while IV infusion is running → overlap for 1–2 hours after oral dose, then taper IV off
  • • Use long-acting agents: amlodipine, carvedilol/metoprolol succinate, ACEi/ARB, chlorthalidone
  • • Avoid abrupt IV discontinuation → rebound hypertension
  • • Monitor BP closely during transition (q15–30min for first 2 hours)
  • • Adherence check: Most hypertensive emergencies are caused by medication nonadherence. Identify barriers (cost, side effects, health literacy), simplify regimen, arrange follow-up within 48–72 hours.

Special Scenarios

Sympathomimetic Toxicity (Cocaine/Amphetamines)

  • • First-line: Benzodiazepines → diazepam 5–10 mg IV or lorazepam 2–4 mg IV. Most respond to sedation alone.
  • • If BP persists: Phentolamine 5 mg IV bolus or nicardipine infusion
  • • Avoid standalone β-blocker unopposed α-vasoconstriction risk
  • • If β-blocker unavoidable (concurrent MI): use labetalol (has α-blocking property)
  • • Hypotension: Norepinephrine (NOT dopamine → worsens arrhythmias)

Severe Preeclampsia / Eclampsia

  • • First-line: IV labetalol 20–40 mg q10min (max 300 mg), IV hydralazine 5–10 mg q20min, or PO nifedipine 10–20 mg
  • • MgSO₄: 4–6 g IV loading → 1–2 g/hr for seizure prophylaxis
  • • Target: SBP <160 mmHg, DBP <110 mmHg
  • • Avoid: ACEi, ARBs, direct renin inhibitors (teratogenic)
  • • Definitive: Delivery of fetus and placenta (postpartum risk persists ≥72h)

MAOI + Tyramine Reaction

  • • Mechanism: MAOI + tyramine-rich food → massive catecholamine release
  • • Antidote: Phentolamine 5 mg IV bolus (α-blocker; repeat q5min if needed)
  • • Alternative: Nicardipine infusion
  • • Prevention: Avoid aged cheese, cured meats, soy sauce, draft beer while on MAOI

Malignant Hypertension

  • • Definition: Severe HTN with Grade III–IV retinopathy (± papilledema)
  • • MAHA: Check schistocytes, thrombocytopenia, ↑LDH → differentiate from TTP/HUS
  • • Treat: Nicardipine or labetalol per standard emergency protocol
  • • Follow-up: Nephrology referral — CKD is common outcome
