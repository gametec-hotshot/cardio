STEMI Equivalents on ECG

ECG patterns that warrant emergent reperfusion therapy — these are STEMI equivalents requiring immediate cath lab activation.

⚡

Wellens Syndrome

Pattern A: Biphasic T waves in V2-V3. Pattern B: Deeply inverted symmetric T waves in V2-V3 (more common, 76%).

Key Points

  • • Indicates critical proximal LAD stenosis
  • • T waves may normalize when pain returns — don't be fooled
  • • Cardiac biomarkers typically normal or minimally elevated
  • • Stress test is contraindicated — can trigger massive MI
  • • Urgent angiography and PCI indicated
⚡

de Winter T Waves

Upsloping ST depression ≥1mm at J point in V1-V6, transitioning to tall prominent symmetric T waves. Often with 0.5-1mm ST elevation in aVR.

Key Points

  • • Represents acute proximal LAD occlusion
  • • Only ~2% of anterior infarctions
  • • No ST elevation in precordial leads — that's the trap
  • • Treat as STEMI: emergent reperfusion
  • • Described in 2008 by de Winter et al. (NEJM)
🔍

Posterior MI

Horizontal ST depression in V1-V3, tall broad R waves (R/S > 1) in V2-V3, upright T waves in leads with terminal S wave. Confirm with posterior leads V7-V9.

Key Points

  • • Horizontal ST depression in V1-V3, NOT sagging
  • • R/S ratio > 1 in V2 = posterior STEMI
  • • Always place V7-V9 (V7: left posterior axillary line, V8: tip of scapula, V9: paraspinal) all 5th intercostal space
  • • ≥0.5mm ST elevation in V7-V9 = positive (≥1mm if age <40)
  • • Often coexists with inferior MI (LCx or RCA occlusion)
💜

LBBB & New/Presumed New LBBB

Use Sgarbossa criteria: Concordant ST elevation ≥1mm (5 pts), Concordant ST depression ≥1mm in V1-V3 (3 pts), Discordant ST elevation ≥5mm (2 pts). Modified: discordant STE with ratio ≥0.25 (3 pts).

Key Points

  • • ≥3 points = STEMI equivalent
  • • Modified Sgarbossa more specific: discordant STE ÷ depth of preceding S wave ratio ≥0.25
  • • New LBBB with symptoms still warrants urgent evaluation
  • • Don't rely on "new vs old" alone — use criteria
📊

Smith Modified STEMI Criteria

For STE in V2-V3: If male <40, ST elevation ≥2.5mm; if male ≥40, ≥2mm; if female, ≥1.5mm. Then apply these rules to distinguish LAD occlusion from other causes.

Key Points

  • • LAD occlusion pattern with hyperacute T waves in precordial leads
  • • Any abnormal Q wave in V2-V3 = pathological
  • • T waves larger than T wave in lead V6 = hyperacute
  • • STE 60ms after J point in V3 < 0.5mm favors occlusion
  • • Smith's formula: 4th power of QTc ÷ amplitude of tallest T wave in V2 < 17.7 = LAD occlusion
🫀

Right Ventricular Infarction

ST elevation in III > II, STE in V1 with V1 STE ÷ V3 STE ratio > 1. Confirm with right-sided leads (V4R most sensitive).

Key Points

  • • V4R: right 5th intercostal space at midclavicular line
  • • ST elevation ≥0.5mm in V4R = RV infarction (sensitivity ~88%, specificity ~78%)
  • • AVOID nitrates and diuretics — preload dependent
  • • Treat with IV fluids to maintain preload
  • • Up to 50% of inferior STEMIs have RV involvement
  • • Look for clear lungs, elevated JVP, hypotension = classic triad

⚠️ Common Drugs That Prolong QTc

High Risk

  • • Amiodarone (Class III antiarrhythmic)
  • • Sotalol (Class III antiarrhythmic)
  • • Dofetilide (Class III antiarrhythmic)
  • • Ibutilide (Class III antiarrhythmic)
  • • Quinidine (Class IA antiarrhythmic)
  • • Haloperidol IV (high dose)
  • • Methadone (≥100 mg/day)
  • • Ondansetron IV (≥32 mg single dose)

Moderate Risk

  • • Fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, levofloxacin)
  • • Macrolides (azithromycin, clarithromycin)
  • • Azole antifungals (fluconazole, voriconazole)
  • • TCAs (amitriptyline, imipramine)
  • • SSRIs (citalopram >40 mg, escitalopram)
  • • Domperidone
  • • Chlorpromazine / Quetiapine
  • • Donepezil
CredibleMeds: For comprehensive drug-induced QT prolongation risk assessment, check crediblemeds.org. Always check for drug-drug interactions before starting medications in patients with baseline QTc >450 ms (M) or >470 ms (F). Risk increases with hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and bradycardia.

🫀 Echocardiography Interpretation Guide

Systematic approach to echo interpretation with key measurements, normal values, and diagnostic algorithms for common cardiac pathologies.

Basic Measurements

Left Ventricular Function

  • LVEF: ≥55% normal, 45-54% mildly reduced, 35-44% moderately reduced, <35% severely reduced
  • LVEDD: 4.2-5.9 cm (male), 3.9-5.3 cm (female)
  • LVESD: 2.7-4.0 cm (male), 2.5-3.8 cm (female)
  • IVSd: 0.6-1.1 cm (septal thickness)
  • PWd: 0.6-1.1 cm (posterior wall thickness)

Right Ventricular Function

  • RV FAC: ≥35% normal
  • TR velocity: <2.8 m/s normal (estimate PASP)
  • RVSP: = 4×(TR Vmax)² + RAP
  • RAP estimate: IVC <2.1 cm and >50% collapse = 3 mmHg

Valvular Assessment

  • Aortic Valve: Vmax <2 m/s normal, >4 m/s severe AS
  • Mitral Valve: E/A ratio, deceleration time
  • AVA: ≥1.0 cm² normal, <1.0 cm² mild AS
  • MR severity: PISA method, vena contracta

Diastolic Function Assessment

Grade I (Impaired Relaxation)

  • E/A < 0.8
  • DT > 200 ms
  • E' septal < 7 cm/s, lateral < 10 cm/s
  • E/E' < 8

Grade II (Pseudonormal)

  • E/A 0.8-1.5
  • DT 160-200 ms
  • E/E' 9-12
  • Use Valsalva or exercise to unmask

Grade III (Restrictive)

  • E/A > 2
  • DT < 160 ms
  • E/E' > 13
  • IVRT < 60 ms

Key Algorithm

  1. Check E/A ratio and E' velocities
  2. Calculate E/E' (elevated = increased LA pressure)
  3. Assess TR velocity and IVC for RA pressure
  4. Integrate with clinical picture

Pericardial Disease

Pericardial Effusion

  • Small: <10 mm echo-free space
  • Moderate: 10-20 mm
  • Large: >20 mm or tamponade physiology
  • Loculated: Consider malignancy or infection

Tamponade Signs

  • • RA/RV diastolic collapse
  • • IVC plethora (>2.1 cm, <50% collapse)
  • • Respiratory variation in MV inflow (>25%)
  • • Pulsus paradoxus clinically

Constrictive Pericarditis

  • • Septal bounce (respiratory shift)
  • • Mitral E' > 8 cm/s with annulus reversus
  • • Hepatic vein expiratory diastolic flow reversal
  • • Thickened pericardium (>4 mm)
Tip: Always assess for tamponade physiology regardless of effusion size. Small effusions can tamponade if loculated.

Systematic Echo Interpretation Algorithm

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. 1
    Overall Impression

    Assess global LV/RV function, chamber sizes, and pericardium

  2. 2
    LV Function & Dimensions

    Measure LVEF, wall thickness, cavity size, regional wall motion

  3. 3
    RV Assessment

    RV size/function, TR jet for PASP, IVC for RA pressure

  4. 4
    Valvular Evaluation

    Stenosis/regurgitation severity, gradients, valve morphology

  5. 5
    Diastolic Function

    Mitral inflow, tissue Doppler, LA pressure estimate

Common Pathologic Patterns

Dilated Cardiomyopathy
  • • LVEDD >5.9 cm (male), >5.3 cm (female)
  • • LVEF <40%
  • • Secondary MR common
  • • Assess for thrombus (apical)
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy
  • • Asymmetric septal hypertrophy
  • • SAM of mitral valve
  • • LVOT gradient assessment
  • • Diastolic dysfunction
Heart Failure with Preserved EF
  • • LVEF ≥50%
  • • Diastolic dysfunction (Grade II/III)
  • • LA enlargement
  • • Elevated E/E' ratio
Aortic Stenosis
  • • AV Vmax >4 m/s (severe)
  • • AVA <1.0 cm² (severe)
  • • Assess LV hypertrophy/function
  • • Check for aortic regurgitation

POCUS Cardiac Views Reference

Parasternal Long Axis (PLAX)

Probe: 3rd-4th ICS, L sternal border. Foot toward L shoulder.

Key structures: LV, LA, AV, MV, aortic root

Parasternal Short Axis (PSAX)

Rotate 90° from PLAX. Clockwise rotation shows valves.

Key structures: RV, LV (circular), valves, papillary muscles

Apical 4-Chamber (A4C)

Probe: 5th ICS, L mid-clavicular line. Apex toward screen.

Key structures: All 4 chambers, MV, TV, septum

Subxiphoid (Subcostal)

Probe: Below xiphoid, angled cephalad. Good for tamponade.

Key structures: IVC, liver, pericardial space, RV/LV

POCUS Protocol for Shock

  1. PLAX: Assess LV function, pericardial effusion
  2. PSAX: LV/RV size comparison, septal motion
  3. A4C: Overall function, chamber sizes
  4. Subcostal: IVC size/collapse, tamponade
  5. Integrate: Hyperdynamic LV + flat IVC = hypovolemia; D-sign = RV strain

⚠️ Dosing Considerations

  • Weight-based dosing: Use ideal body weight (IBW) for obese patients to avoid overdosing
  • Renal adjustment: Most cardiac drugs require dose reduction when CrCl <30 mL/min
  • Hepatic impairment: Reduce doses of metabolized drugs (amiodarone, carvedilol) in liver disease
  • Drug interactions: Check for CYP450 interactions, especially with antiarrhythmics
  • Elderly patients: Start at lower doses, titrate slowly due to comorbidities
  • Pregnancy: Avoid ACEI/ARB in pregnancy, use heparin for anticoagulation

🧪 Electrolyte Management in Cardiac Patients

Comprehensive guidelines for electrolyte imbalances in cardiovascular disease, including monitoring, treatment, and drug interactions in heart failure, arrhythmias, and acute coronary syndromes.

Potassium Management

Hyperkalemia (K⁺ >5.5 mEq/L)

  • ECG Changes: Peaked T waves, flattened P waves, widened QRS, sine wave
  • Acute Treatment: Calcium gluconate 10% (10 mL IV) for cardiac protection
  • Shift Therapy: Insulin + glucose (10U regular insulin + 50g dextrose)
  • Elimination: Kayexalate (15g PO/PR), loop diuretics, dialysis
  • ACEI/ARB: Hold if K⁺ >5.0, restart at lower dose when normalized

Hypokalemia (K⁺ <3.5 mEq/L)

  • ECG Changes: Flattened T waves, U waves, ST depression, PVCs
  • Risk: Increased risk of torsades de pointes with QT prolongation
  • Replacement: Oral KCl (20-40 mEq/day), IV KCl (10-20 mEq/hr max)
  • Monitoring: Daily BMP, ECG if symptomatic
  • Causes in HF: Diuretics, low intake, GI losses, alkalosis

Drug Interactions

  • ↑ K⁺: ACEI/ARB, K⁺-sparing diuretics, heparin, NSAIDs, trimethoprim
  • ↓ K⁺: Loop/thiazide diuretics, corticosteroids, amphotericin, insulin
  • Monitoring: Check BMP 48-72h after starting/frequency changes

Magnesium Management

Hypermagnesemia (Mg²⁺ >2.6 mEq/L)

  • Symptoms: Nausea, weakness, bradycardia, hypotension
  • ECG: Prolonged PR/AV block, widened QRS, asystole
  • Treatment: Calcium gluconate IV, IV fluids, dialysis if severe
  • Common in HF: With renal dysfunction + Mg-containing laxatives

Hypomagnesemia (Mg²⁺ <1.7 mEq/L)

  • ECG Changes: QT prolongation, torsades de pointes, atrial fibrillation
  • Supplementation: MgSO₄ 1-2g IV, then oral magnesium oxide 400mg BID
  • Diuretic-Induced: Common with loop/thiazides, replace Mg with K supplementation
  • Digitalis Toxicity: Hypomagnesemia potentiates digoxin toxicity

Clinical Applications

  • AF with RVR: 1-2g MgSO₄ IV for rate control
  • Torsades: 1-2g MgSO₄ IV bolus, then infusion
  • AMI: Magnesium supplementation may reduce arrhythmias
  • Monitoring: Serum Mg levels with renal function

Calcium & Phosphate Management

Hypercalcemia (Ca²⁺ >10.5 mg/dL)

  • ECG Changes: Shortened QT interval, Osborn waves (hypothermia)
  • Treatment: IV fluids (NS), loop diuretics, bisphosphonates, calcitonin
  • Cardiac Effects: Bradycardia, AV block, digitalis toxicity potentiation
  • Causes: Hyperparathyroidism, malignancy, thiazides, vitamin D toxicity

Hypocalcemia (Ca²⁺ <8.5 mg/dL)

  • ECG Changes: Prolonged QT interval, T wave inversion
  • Treatment: IV calcium gluconate (1-2g), oral calcium carbonate
  • Risk: Increased risk of torsades, especially with hypomagnesemia
  • Post-Cardiac Surgery: Monitor ionized calcium closely

Phosphate Disorders

  • Hyperphosphatemia: Seen in renal failure, treat with phosphate binders
  • Hypophosphatemia: Refeeding syndrome, alcoholism, may cause HF exacerbation
  • Cardiac Effects: Myocardial dysfunction, arrhythmias
  • Monitoring: Essential in advanced HF with malnutrition

Special Considerations

Heart Failure Management

  • ACEI/ARB: Monitor K⁺/Cr weekly for 2 weeks, then monthly
  • Spironolactone: K⁺ monitoring critical (hold if K⁺ >5.0)
  • Loop Diuretics: Monitor K⁺, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺ depletion
  • Cardiorenal Syndrome: Adjust electrolyte goals with renal function

Arrhythmia Prevention

  • QT Prolonging Drugs: Maintain K⁺ 4.0-5.0, Mg²⁺ 2.0-2.5 mEq/L
  • Antiarrhythmic Therapy: Amiodarone requires thyroid/electrolyte monitoring
  • Pacemaker Patients: Avoid severe electrolyte imbalances
  • Post-Cardioversion: Monitor for electrolyte shifts

Acute Coronary Syndromes

  • STEMI: Hypokalemia may occur with catecholamine surge
  • Cardiogenic Shock: Monitor ionized calcium, treat aggressively
  • Post-PCI: Heparin monitoring, avoid heparin-induced hyperkalemia
  • Statin Therapy: Monitor CK, liver enzymes; rare rhabdomyolysis

Monitoring Frequency

Heart Failure Patients
  • • BMP: Weekly ×2 after ACEI/ARB/aldosterone antagonist initiation
  • • Monthly thereafter if stable
  • • More frequent with diuretic dose changes
  • • Magnesium: With loop diuretic use
Antiarrhythmic Therapy
  • • QT prolonging drugs: K⁺/Mg²⁺ monitoring required
  • • Amiodarone: TFTs, LFTs, CXR baseline
  • • Digoxin: Levels 6-8h post-dose, renal function
  • • Antiarrhythmic monitoring: ECG, electrolytes
Critical Care Setting
  • • Ionized calcium: Hourly in cardiogenic shock
  • • ABG/electrolytes: Every 2-4h in ICU
  • • Phosphate: Daily in refeeding/nutrition support
  • • Magnesium: With TPN or prolonged fasting

⚠️ Key Clinical Pearls

  • Triple Therapy Risk: ACEI + K⁺-sparing diuretic + ARB = high hyperkalemia risk (avoid)
  • Digoxin Toxicity: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypercalcemia increase risk
  • Diuretic Resistance: Check for hypokalemia, which impairs diuretic efficacy
  • Post-Cardiac Surgery: Ionized hypocalcemia common, treat to maintain cardiac function
  • Refeeding Syndrome: Phosphate supplementation critical to prevent cardiac dysfunction
  • Renal Adjustment: All electrolyte replacement must account for GFR

🫀 Echocardiography Interpretation Guide

Systematic approach to interpreting transthoracic echocardiograms with key views, measurements, and clinical correlations.

Standard Echocardiographic Views

Parasternal Long Axis (PLAX)

Displays LV, LA, aortic valve, mitral valve, and aortic root. Best for LV dimensions and aortic measurements.

Parasternal Short Axis (PSAX)

Shows circular cross-sections at different levels (mitral, papillary, apex). Essential for regional wall motion analysis.

Apical 4-Chamber (A4C)

Visualizes all 4 chambers, tricuspid and mitral valves, interventricular septum. Used for EF calculation and valve assessment.

Apical 2-Chamber (A2C)

Shows LV and LA without RV. Useful for anterior and inferior wall assessment.

Apical 3-Chamber (A3C)

Long axis view of LV outflow tract, aortic valve, and mitral valve. Ideal for aortic stenosis evaluation.

Subcostal and Suprasternal

Alternative windows for patients with poor acoustic windows. Subcostal for IVC, abdominal aorta; Suprasternal for aortic arch.

Valve Assessment

Mitral Valve

  • Stenosis: mean gradient >5 mmHg, valve area <1.0 cm²
  • Regurgitation: color Doppler shows regurgitant jet
  • Prolapse: leaflets bow into LA in systole

Aortic Valve

  • Stenosis: peak velocity >2.5 m/s, mean gradient >20 mmHg
  • Regurgitation: diastolic flow reversal in descending aorta
  • Bicuspid: two cusps instead of three

Tricuspid Valve

  • Regurgitation: common in pulmonary hypertension
  • Assess RV function and pulmonary pressures

Pulmonic Valve

  • Rarely affected, assess for stenosis or regurgitation
  • Peak velocity <2 m/s normal

Doppler Principles

Doppler echocardiography uses ultrasound to assess blood flow velocity and direction.

Pulsed Wave (PW) Doppler

Measures velocity at a specific point. Used for mitral inflow, LVOT velocities.

Continuous Wave (CW) Doppler

Measures highest velocity along the beam. Used for valve gradients.

Color Doppler

Displays flow direction and turbulence. Red = flow towards probe, Blue = away.

Tissue Doppler

Assesses myocardial velocity for diastolic function.

Common Pathological Findings

Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

Septal or posterior wall thickness >1.1 cm. Causes: hypertension, aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.

Left Ventricular Dysfunction

EF <50%. Assess wall motion abnormalities, regional vs global dysfunction.

Valvular Heart Disease

Stenosis: increased gradients, reduced valve area. Regurgitation: regurgitant fraction, jet size.

Pericardial Effusion

Echo-free space around heart. Tamponade: diastolic RV collapse, respiratory variation in mitral inflow.

Cardiac Masses

Thrombus: in LA appendage (AF), LV apex. Tumors: myxoma in LA.

Congenital Defects

ASD: drop-out in septum. VSD: color flow across septum. PDA: turbulent flow in PA.
